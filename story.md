# THE TALE OF SIDI NUMAN

“COMMANDER OF THE FAITHFUL,” said Sidi Numan, “I shall pass over my birth; it is too
modest to merit Your Majesty’s attention. As for worldly goods, my ancestors were
careful people, and thanks to them, I am able to live as a gentleman, without debt or
labor.
Being so fortunate, all I might have wanted to complete my happiness was a decent
wife who could return my affection, but I was not granted that blessing. Instead, I was
cursed with a bride who, from the very first day of our marriage, began to try my

patience in ways unimaginable to any who had not suffered what I had.^22
As custom requires that we marry without so much as a glance^23 at the woman we
are to spend our lives with, Your Majesty knows that a man has no reason to complain
as long as his bride is not repulsive or misshapen, or rather, as long as a sound mind
and pleasant manners make up for any blemishes she may have.
The first time I saw my wife uncovered, when they brought her to my home with the
usual ceremonies, I was delighted to find that I had not been misled about her beauty.
She was just to my taste. We were going to be happy.
The following day, a feast was laid for us, but I found myself waiting alone at the
table. At length my wife appeared, and as we took our seats, I drew a veil over my


impatience. First I helped myself to rice, in the usual way, with a spoon. My wife,
shunning the spoon, instead produced from her pocket a case, out of which came a sort
of earpick with which she proceeded to lift the rice to her mouth one grain at a time,^24
for it could carry no more.
Alarmed by this way of eating, I said, “Amina (for that was her name), is this how
you eat rice at home? Is it because you are a small eater, or do you count the grains so
as never to eat more one day than the next? Is it waste that worries you? If you are trying
to teach me a lesson in thrift, have no fear: We shall never ruin ourselves that way.
Thanks to heaven we have enough to live comfortably, so please do not deprive
yourself, dear Amina, and eat as you see me eat.”
I expected my good-natured chiding to prompt a response in kind, but she continued
to pick at the rice in that way of hers without saying a word, leaving longer and longer
gaps between mouthfuls just to upset me. When I moved on to the other dishes, she
restricted herself to a few crumbs, no more than a sparrow would have taken, which she
carried to her mouth at odd intervals.

Her mulishness appalled me.^25 But I decided, out of mercy, that she was perhaps not
used to eating in the presence of men, least of all a husband, and that she had simply
taken the notion of wifely restraint a little too literally. Perhaps she had already eaten,
or perhaps she was holding back so as to eat alone, unhindered, later. These
deliberations stopped me from saying anything that might have upset her further. After
lunch, I took my leave exactly as I would have done, had she not profoundly disturbed
me with her strange behavior, and withdrew.
Dinner was no different. The following day, and every time we ate together, the
same thing occurred. It was clear to me that no woman could survive on the crumbs she
fed herself, and that something mysterious was at work. Dissimulation became my ruse.
I pretended not to notice her conduct, in the hope that, with time, she might reconcile
herself to life with me. But my hope was in vain, and I would soon be convinced of it.
One night, believing me asleep, Amina rose softly and dressed in silence, taking
great care not to wake me. I could not imagine what had stirred her from her bed, and,
curious to find out, I made a show of sinking further into sleep. She stole out of the room
without a sound.
A moment later, I was up, robed, and at the window, in time to see her cross the
courtyard, open the door, and disappear into the street.
I ran to the door, which she had left ajar, and followed her moonlit form to the
cemetery. Hiding in the shadows, I saw my wife in the company of a female ghoul.^26
I need hardly remind Your Majesty that ghouls are demons of either sex that roam
the countryside at night. They make their home in ruins, and they like to throw
themselves on passersby to kill them and feed on their flesh. When no one passes by,
they go to the cemetery to dig up the dead and feast on their bodies instead.


I was filled with horror at the sight of my wife with the ghoul. They dug up a body
that had not been buried longer than a day, and the ghoul hacked at its flesh, and they ate
the hunks of meat together,^27 sitting on the edge of the pit. They appeared to keep up a
perfectly calm conversation throughout their inhuman meal, but I was too far off to hear

a word of their exchange, which must have been as strange as their food.^28 It chills me
still to recall it.
When they had finished their horrible dinner, they flung the remains of the corpse
into the grave and covered it with earth. I left them to it and hurried home, where I made
sure to leave the door ajar as I had found it, climbed back into bed, and pretended once
more to sleep.
Before long, Amina returned as soundlessly as she had left,^29 undressed, and
slipped into bed, no doubt congratulating herself on having escaped my notice.
Haunted by the barbaric act I had just witnessed, and horrified to find myself in bed
with the woman who had performed it, I struggled to find sleep, and when I did, it was
so light that the first call to prayer was enough to wake me. I dressed and went to the
mosque.
After prayers, I left the city and spent the day roaming the gardens, pondering how

best to compel my wife to change her ways. I rejected all the violent measures^30 that
suggested themselves to me, and I resolved to wean her off her terrible habit through
gentleness alone. These thoughts escorted me all the way home, where I happened to
arrive in time for dinner.
As soon as I appeared, Amina called for the meal to be served, and we took our
places at the table. Since she persisted in picking at her rice, as gently as possible I
said, “Amina, surely you remember my surprise when, the day after our wedding, you
declined to eat anything but a few grains of rice, in such a way that any other husband
would have been appalled. I did nothing but tell you how it saddened me and point out
the variety of dishes before us in the hope of tempting your appetite. Since that day, our
meals have changed, but your attitude remains the same. I have kept quiet until now, and
I do not mean to upset you, but please tell me, Amina, do you really prefer the taste of
dead flesh?”
Hardly were these words out when Amina, who understood that I had spied on her,
was gripped by a dreadful fury. Her cheeks burned, her eyes bulged in their sockets, and
she foamed at the mouth with rage.
I watched her in horror, unable to move, dreading to think what she was plotting
against me. In the heat of her anger, she seized a jug that stood nearby and, plunging her
hands inside, muttered a few strange words and flung its water at my face.^31
“Creep!” she cried. “To punish you for prying, become a dog!”
As these words tumbled from her mouth, I suddenly knew two things: that Amina

was a magician,^32 and that I was no longer a man.

I was too stunned by my brutal transformation to think of running away, and Amina
took the opportunity to beat me with a stick, striking me so hard that it was sheer luck I

escaped with my life. I tried to shake her off, but she hunted me into the courtyard^33 with
the same mad fury, and no matter how nimbly I ran this way and that, I was not deft
enough to evade her blows and had to suffer many more. At last, tired by the chase and
desperate to finish me off once and for all, she pulled the door ajar so as to crush me


with it when I tried to pass. Dog though I was, I saw through her malicious plan. The
presence of danger quickened my mind, and I was able to time my movements so well
that I caught her off guard and slipped out to freedom, unharmed except for a scrape on
the tip of my tail.
It hurt, and I howled so noisily all along the streets that the other dogs came out and
bit me. To avoid them, I took refuge in a butcher’s shop, where sheep’s heads, tongues,
and trotters were sold.
My host came zealously to my defense, driving off the dogs that were at my heels
while I crept into a corner out of sight. Yet his shop was not quite the haven I had hoped
for. He was one of those fanatics who hold all dogs to be unclean,^34 and for whom all
the soap and water in the world will not suffice if a dog happens to brush past them.
Once my attackers had retreated, he tried to force me out as well, but I would not move
from my corner, and I spent the night recovering from Amina’s abuse.
I will not tire Your Majesty with the sad thoughts that gripped me in my new state.
Allow me simply to skip to the following morning, when my host returned from the
market laden with sheep’s heads, tongues, and trotters, which he laid out for the day’s
trade. Stepping out from my hiding place, I noticed that the smell of meat had attracted a
number of neighborhood dogs, which now arranged themselves outside the shop,
waiting to be fed. I crept out and stood with them.
My host, knowing I had eaten nothing since the day before, made a point of throwing
me bigger and better bits than those that fell to the other dogs. When he had finished, I
begged to be allowed back in, but he was unmoved by my pleading looks and wagging
tail, and he stood firmly at the door, holding a stick, until I was forced to retreat.
A few doors down, I came upon a bakery. Unlike the melancholy vendor of sheep’s
heads, the baker seemed a cheerful man who at that moment was having his breakfast.
Though I gave no sign of hunger, he at once threw me a piece of bread. Instead of

lunging at it in the manner of other dogs, I first bowed my head at him^35 and wagged my
tail in thanks. He repaid the courtesy with a smile. I had no need to eat, but I took the
piece of bread all the same and chewed it slowly, to show him I was doing it out of
politeness. He noticed all this and seemed not to mind my presence at his shop. I sat
still, facing the street, to prove that I only asked for his protection.
This he gave me, and he even stroked my back until I felt bold enough to step inside,
though I made it clear that I was only entering with his permission. He welcomed me in
and showed me a corner where I could sit out of his way. There I stayed throughout my
time at the bakery, where I was treated with unfailing kindness, and my host never broke
bread without sharing it with me. I thanked him with my devotion.^36
My eyes never left him, and I shadowed his every step. He seemed to enjoy the
attention, so I redoubled my efforts. Sometimes, leaving the house, he would cry out the
name he had chosen for me: “Ginger!”


The sound of it made me leap. Out into the street I went, yapping and frisking in the
doorway until he appeared. I would tag along on his errands, never more than a few
steps ahead or behind, gazing up at him now and then to show my delight.
I had been living there for some time when a woman came in one day to buy bread.
To pay for it, she handed over a false coin mixed in among the good ones. The baker
perceived this, gave it back, and asked for another in its place.
The woman refused, insisting it was genuine, but the baker held his ground.
“This coin is so clearly fake,” he said at last, “that even my dog would not be
fooled. Ginger!”
I hopped onto the counter, and the baker laid out the change.
“See if you can find the bad coin.”
I inspected each in turn, and, setting my paw down on the false one, looked up at my
master in triumph.
The baker, who had only been joking, was startled by my ability, and the woman
was forced to admit defeat and produce another coin in its place. As soon as she was
gone, my master called his neighbors and told them, with many embellishments, what I
had done.
The skeptical neighbors set many coins before me, but without fail my paw came
down on the false ones and set them apart from the others.
The woman, meanwhile, told everyone she met about our encounter. My skill for
sniffing out false money became the talk of the neighborhood. Soon it had spread
through the town.
My days were busy. All those who bought bread from my master asked for proof of
my gift. People came from the farthest quarters of the city just to see me perform. My
fame drew such crowds that the baker struggled to meet demand. He told his friends that
I was worth my weight in gold.
Admiration was soon followed by envy.^37 Traps were laid to abduct me, and the
baker never dared let me out of his sight. One day, a woman came in, like the rest, to ask
for bread. My bed was now up on the counter. She threw down six coins, one of which
was false. I picked it out at once, trapping it under my paw and cocking my head as if to
ask whether I was mistaken.
“That’s right,” said the woman. “That’s the one.”
She stood there holding my gaze for a long time, then paid for her bread. As she
stepped out, she motioned for me to follow.
Half my thoughts were always turned to some means of escape from my strange
condition. The woman’s insistent look had struck me. I guessed that she might have
divined something of my misfortune, and in this I was proven right. But I held back at
first, watching her. She turned and, seeing that I had not moved, beckoned to me once
more.


I resolved to go. The baker was busy with his oven and paid me no attention. I crept
out after the woman and trailed her home.
“Come in,” she said, holding open the door. “You will not be sorry that you
followed me.”
Once we had entered, she locked the door and led me into a room, where a beautiful
young woman was working at a piece of embroidery. She was the daughter of the kind
woman who had brought me here,^38 and, as I was soon to learn, was highly skilled in
the art of magic.
“My daughter,” said the woman, “I have brought you the baker’s dog, whose tricks
are on everyone’s lips. You remember that when I first heard of him, I wondered
whether he was really a man that some evil force had turned into a dog. Today I went to
buy bread and saw for myself the truth of the story, and I was able to persuade the most-
talked-about dog in Baghdad to follow me home. What do you think?”
“Your guess was right, Mother. Let me show you.”
She rose and reached for a jug, dipped a hand in it, and sprinkled my face with its

water.^39
“If you were born a dog,” she said, “remain a dog. But if you were born a man, let
this water restore your human form.”
The spell was broken at once. The dog vanished and I stood before her as a man.
Overcome with joy, I threw myself at the young woman’s feet and kissed the hem of
her dress.
“My savior!” I said. “I cannot thank you enough except to give myself to you to deal
with as you wish. I am yours.”
I told her my whole story (my long-suffering marriage with Amina, her strange
moods and manners, her incredible act of aggression) and thanked her mother once more
for the happiness she had brought me.
“Sidi Numan,” replied the daughter, “say no more of your gratitude. The knowledge
that we have been of service to a gentleman like you is recompense enough. Let us
speak of Amina, your wife, whom I happened to know before your marriage. We studied
the art of magic under the same mistress. We even met going to the same baths, but we
never got along, and we were careful to avoid each other’s company. Her mean-spirited
curse does not surprise me. But my antidote is not complete. It is not enough to have
reversed the spell; she must also be punished for her malice, and you must reclaim your
authority.^40 Stay here with my mother one moment.”
As she disappeared behind a door, I took the chance to heap thanks on her mother
once again.
“As you can see,” she said, “my daughter is as gifted a magician as Amina herself,

but she uses her talent to help others.^41 That is why I let her do it. If I thought she was
abusing her power, I would have put a stop to it long ago.”


The mother had just launched into an account of one of the marvelous deeds she had
witnessed when her daughter returned, holding a small vial.
“Sidi Numan,” she said, “my books tell me^42 that Amina is not home at present but
is due to return at any moment. I have also learned that she makes a great show of
worrying about your absence in front of the servants. She has been telling people that,
while at dinner with her, you suddenly remembered some important business that
required you to leave at once, and that a dog had strayed in through the open door, and it
had had to be chased out with a stick. Go home, then, without delay, and take this vial.
Amina won’t be long. As soon as she enters, go down to meet her in the courtyard. She

will try to run away, so be quick. Throw this water over her^43 and say, ‘Be punished for
your crimes!’ You shall see what happens next.”
My ears rang with her words as I sped home, after having sworn my eternal
gratitude once more. Everything happened exactly as the young magician had predicted.
Amina arrived home before long and I went down to meet her, vial in hand. She gave a
shriek and was already turning on her heels when I flung the water at her face and spoke
the magic words. Amina disappeared, and in her place stood the horse Your Majesty
saw yesterday.
I seized her at once by the mane and dragged her to the stables, where I slipped a
halter around her neck^44 and tied her up. There I cursed her for her cruelty and whipped
her until my strength gave up. But I took up the whip again the next day, and every day
after that.

I hope, Commander of the Faithful,^45 you will not take exception to my conduct, and
will not think this wicked woman more harshly treated than she deserves.

“SIDI NUMAN,” replied the caliph, “your story is a strange one, and there are no excuses
for your wife. Though I do not wholly condemn your treatment of her,^46 I wish you
would consider how painful it is to be changed to an animal, and let that punishment be
enough. I am tempted to have you ask the young witch to reverse the spell and change
your wife back to her human form, but I know that witches can be cruel and stubborn,
and I fear that she would inflict on you a harder fate than you have had before.”
Then the caliph, gentle and full of pity for those who suffer, turned to the third man
his vizier had brought before him.
“Khawaja Hasan,” he said, “as I passed yesterday before your house and found it
splendid, I wished to know who owned it. I learned that you had built it, though your
profession scarcely earned you enough to live. I was also told that you made good use
of the fortune God had given you, and that you were beloved by your neighbors. All this
pleased me, and persuaded me that fate has blessed you with this wealth in some
unusual way. Open your heart and tell me all, so that I may take pleasure in your
happiness with greater understanding. You have my assurance that your property is
safe.”
At these calming words, Khawaja Hasan threw himself down before the throne,
touched his forehead to the ground, and rose to face the caliph.
“Anyone but me,” he said, “might have been troubled to receive an order to appear
before Your Majesty. But since I have only ever borne you deep respect, and have never
done a thing to break the law, my only fear was that the splendor of your throne would
stun me into silence. Still, having heard how kindly Your Majesty receives and listens to
your subjects, I had no doubt that in your presence I would have the courage and the
confidence to give you what you seek. You have been good enough to give me your
protection, and I hope you will not change your mind when you have heard my story.”
Then Khawaja Hasan began his tale.
